Notice historique — 1869

In the year the Suez Canal opened, a trunk-maker set up at 175, Boulevard Haussmann.

Lavoët was founded in Paris in 1869, at the exact moment the world learned to move: the great railway termini were rising, the steamship lines were drawing their routes across the Atlantic, and a certain kind of traveller required luggage built like architecture. From its workshop and showroom on the Boulevard Haussmann, the house supplied that traveller — quietly, and for decades.

While its neighbours grew into global maisons, Lavoët never diluted into a fashion brand. It remained a malletier in the strict sense — English trunks, wicker and rush trunks, hat trunks, wardrobe trunks with fitted hangers — each piece carrying the small brass plaque stamped with the address. The house eventually fell silent; the plaques did not. Today its surviving trunks rank among the rarest French luggage in existence, identified at a glance by a pair of painted stripes: one green, one red.

La toile aux trèfles — circa 1920

The house canvas

By the twenties the house had drawn its own cipher — a scattering of gilt trefoils strewn across a chocolate ground, like clover pressed into the canvas — sealed under a coating made for the deck of a steamer. Bound in lozine and beech, trimmed in pigskin: three materials, one signature.

La plaque — signature de la maison

Every genuine Lavoët carries its address. The plates vary — brass on the early trunks, cream enamel stamped MALLES LAVOET between the wars — but the address never does. Where other houses signed with a monogram, this one signed with a place.

Provenance — pièces survivantes

What survives, travels still

Because the house never relaunched, no two surviving Lavoët trunks are alike — each carries its own itinerary of shipping labels, port tags and hotel stickers. Authentic pieces surface through the great antique platforms and a small circle of specialist French restorers, who read a trunk's history the way an archivist reads marginalia.

Collectors verify a piece by its address plate, the hand of its painted stripes, and the hardware stamps beneath the lid. A few trunks signed Au Touriste carry the very same trefoil canvas — a workshop kinship the connoisseurs still debate.
